# Designing AI-Assisted Editorial Feedback

<OrgPill org="reedsy" />

Reedsy operates tools used by thousands of authors and editors working on book manuscripts.

As writing tools market either shifts or expands into AI-assistive technologies, Reedsy started exploring meaningful ways how AI could assist their audience.

I joined as a design consultant to lead the AI product exploration phase and help define what could augment the writing experience, focusing on producing meaningful structural feedback on long-form text.

---

## Problem

Writers struggle to evaluate their manuscripts objectively.

Typical issues include:

- difficulty identifying structural weaknesses
- lack of visibility into narrative pacing
- inconsistent themes or emotional arcs
- limited access to professional editorial feedback

Editors solve these problems through deep reading and experience, but that process is expensive and slow.

The challenge was to explore whether AI could assist with early-stage manuscript analysis, helping writers understand their work before seeking professional editing.

---

## Research

The exploration started with understanding how editors actually analyze manuscripts.

Through conversations with editors and writers, several recurring patterns emerged.

Editors tend to evaluate manuscripts across multiple dimensions:

- narrative structure
- pacing across chapters
- thematic consistency
- character development
- emotional trajectory

Importantly, editorial feedback is rarely about individual sentences. It focuses on patterns across the entire manuscript.

This insight shaped the direction of the project: if AI could identify patterns across long text, it might help surface structural insights.

---

## AI Exploration

Instead of starting with UI, I began with experimentation.

Using LLM prototypes and NLP techniques, I explored ways AI could analyze long-form text and produce structured feedback.

Experiments focused on:

- chapter-level analysis
- manuscript-level patterns
- theme clustering
- emotional tone distribution
- structural inconsistencies

The goal was to understand what kind of insights AI could generate reliably and how those insights might help writers improve their work.

These early prototypes helped determine which signals were meaningful and which were noise.

---

## Visualization Concepts

Raw AI output is rarely helpful on its own. The next step was exploring how those insights could be visualized in ways writers could actually use.

Several visual directions were explored:

Narrative structure visualizations

Concepts for visualizing how themes or narrative elements evolve across chapters.

These views helped writers understand whether ideas appear consistently or disappear unexpectedly.

Emotional progression

Explorations of how emotional tone shifts throughout a manuscript.

This type of visualization helps reveal pacing issues or unexpected tonal shifts.

Structural feedback cards

Concepts for presenting AI insights as editorial-style observations rather than technical output.

For example:

- chapters with similar thematic patterns
- sections with sudden tonal changes
- areas where pacing slows down

The design focus was making AI feedback feel editorial rather than mechanical.

---

## Prototyping

To test these ideas quickly, I built a series of prototypes combining AI output with interactive visual models.

The prototypes allowed the team to evaluate:

- which insights writers found meaningful
- how AI feedback should be presented
- how much information was useful vs overwhelming

This stage was critical because AI systems often produce non-deterministic outputs, and the interface must help users interpret them.

---

## Key Design Principles

Several principles emerged during the work.

AI should augment editors, not replace them

The system should help writers understand their manuscript earlier in the process, not claim to replace professional editing.

Feedback should focus on patterns

Line-level suggestions are less useful for long-form writing than structural insights.

Visualizations make complex analysis understandable

Narrative patterns become easier to interpret when visualized rather than described.

AI output must feel editorial

Writers trust feedback more when it resembles human editorial insight rather than algorithmic scoring.

---

## Outcome

The exploration helped shape the direction of Reedsy’s AI initiatives around long-form writing.

The work demonstrated that AI could generate meaningful manuscript-level insights and helped define how those insights might be surfaced to writers.

The project also contributed to the company’s broader AI exploration efforts, which supported securing a €2M public AI innovation grant.

⸻

## Reflection

Designing AI tools requires a different mindset from traditional product design.

Instead of designing deterministic workflows, the challenge becomes designing interfaces that help users interpret probabilistic outputs.

This project reinforced an important idea:

AI products are not just about generating output.
They are about helping users understand complex information in new ways.

For writers, the goal is not replacing creativity, but providing new lenses through which they can see their work.
