When I joined Reedsy as a consulting product design lead, I saw a company environment where the product culture did not reward small iterations, and hypotheses were not tied to metrics.

As I worked on the Reedsy's ~Narrative Copilot~, I explored ways to integrate AI-assisted workflows into a writing product for authors. The existing product value behind the subscription wasn't compelling enough to drive sustainable growth.

---

## The problem

The business problem was straightforward: subscriptions were not strong enough, and existing premium value was not compelling enough to drive sustainable growth.

In creative tools, this is dangerous. Users can like the product, even praise it, and still never pay. Writing products are full of good intentions, abandoned drafts, and aspirational users. That means a subscription offer cannot rely on vague promises like “write better” or “stay motivated.” It has to deliver clear, felt value inside the actual act of writing.

The market was also shifting fast. AI writing tools were becoming more common, but most of them were converging on the same pattern: generate text, rewrite text, summarize text, repeat. That created a temptation to compete on surface-level features. I did not think that was the right move.

For authors, especially long-form writers, the real problem is usually not “give me more text.” It is understanding whether the text works. Is the pacing off? Is a chapter emotionally flat? Is a character disappearing from the narrative? Is the tone drifting? Are important themes developing or collapsing?

That led to a different product direction. Instead of treating AI as a text vending machine, I explored how it could act more like a reflective layer on top of the manuscript: something that helps writers inspect, evaluate, and improve their work.

### Role

I worked across:

- product thinking,
- workflow design,
- prototyping,
- interface design, and
- system design.

This included framing problems, exploring concepts, designing interaction patterns, and helping translate ambiguous AI capabilities into usable product experiences.

A large part of the work was making non-deterministic AI behavior feel structured enough for a creative workflow. That is the hidden design problem in many AI products. The model can produce something interesting, but the user still needs to understand what it means, whether to trust it, and what to do next.

I was not just drawing screens. I was shaping how AI output should become an action.

## Research and framing

Because the usual metrics culture was weak, I leaned more heavily on qualitative understanding and structured framing. That meant looking closely at the writing process itself, the friction points that make people stall, and the gap between what authors say they want and what actually helps them move forward.

One useful shift was to stop thinking in terms of generic AI assistance and instead focus on moments of uncertainty in the writing journey. Writers do not need help in the abstract. They need help when they are stuck, doubtful, or too close to the text to judge it clearly.

This led me to frame AI value around feedback workflows rather than pure generation workflows.

That distinction mattered a lot. Generation can feel magical, but it often weakens ownership and trust in long-form creative work. Feedback, analysis, and guidance fit better with the psychology of authorship. They support the writer without replacing the writer.

From there, I explored product directions that could surface higher-level signals from a manuscript. Not just line edits or sentence suggestions, but broader narrative patterns: emotional movement, character presence, thematic consistency, and chapter-level diagnostics. The goal was to help writers see their manuscript from a new angle.

## Design approach

The design challenge was to make complex analysis feel useful rather than academic.

Writers do not want to read a dashboard for the sake of reading a dashboard. Every screen needed to answer one question: “What should I do next?”

That led me toward workflows that combined insight with direction. Instead of dumping raw AI observations onto the user, I explored ways to package them into guided interpretation. A good AI-assisted workflow does not end at “here is what we found.” It continues into “here is what this may mean” and “here is how you could respond.”

This affected both interaction design and visual design.

On the interaction side, I looked for ways to keep the experience conversational, inspectable, and chunked into manageable pieces. Writers should be able to move from a high-level signal into specific supporting evidence, then back out again without losing context.

On the visual side, I explored ways to represent narrative information so it felt legible and emotionally intuitive. Long-form writing is abstract. Good visualization can make it concrete. A chart, map, or structured panel can help a writer notice patterns they would not catch from text alone.

This was not about making analytics for the sake of analytics. It was about creating moments of recognition. The best reaction is not “nice UI.” It is “wait, that’s exactly what feels wrong in this chapter.”

## Working inside a difficult product culture

One of the hardest parts of this project was the operating environment.

When small iterations are undervalued and metrics are not a strong decision-making tool, product development gets pulled toward opinion, narrative, and bigger swings. That makes design harder, because essentially, there are no learning loops. You cannot easily say: “let's test this small change, and see if it improves activation”.

In that environment, I found that design had to do more of the strategic heavy lifting. I had to make concepts tangible early, reduce ambiguity through prototypes, and create artifacts that aligned people around a more concrete product direction.

This also changed how I thought about speed. Fast did not mean shipping random experiments. Fast meant reducing vagueness before implementation. Prototypes became a way to test whether an AI-assisted idea even made sense as a user experience before the team committed too much engineering time.

That was one of the biggest lessons from the work: in AI products, prototype quality is strategy. The feature probably does not deserve implementation if there's no legible value in a prototype.

## Outcome

The work helped establish a stronger direction for AI-assisted writing workflows centered on analysis, feedback, and manuscript understanding rather than generic text generation alone.

It also showed that AI could be positioned as a premium layer of value inside the writing experience, not just as a novelty. That mattered from a subscription perspective. Users are more likely to pay for clarity, progress, and confidence in their work than for one more prompt box.

Just as importantly, the project sharpened my view on what makes AI products real. It is not enough to attach a model to a workflow. You have to design trust, interpretation, and action around it.

## What I learned

This project taught me three things.

First, subscription value in creative tools has to be specific. “AI-powered” is not value. “Help me understand whether my chapter works” is closer.

Second, when company culture is weak on iteration and metrics, design must become sharper at framing, prototyping, and reducing ambiguity. Otherwise the product drifts into feature theater.

Third, the best AI workflows often do not try to replace the user. They help the user see better, decide better, and continue with more confidence.

That is the kind of AI product work I want to keep doing: not AI as decoration, and not AI as autopilot, but AI as a well-designed thinking partner.
